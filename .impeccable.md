## Design Context

### Users
The primary users are performance-minded open-source developers and platform/search engineers evaluating whether BORSUK is technically credible. They need to judge recall, latency, query I/O, graph I/O, RSS pressure, resident metadata, ingest/compaction cost, and reproducibility from evidence rather than positioning copy.

### Brand Personality
BORSUK should feel precise, durable, and systems-oriented. The interface should create confidence by exposing constraints, commands, artifact provenance, and failure modes plainly. Claims should be conservative: measured data is labeled as measured, local attempts are labeled with their stop limits, and unmeasured targets are not presented as results.

### Aesthetic Direction
Use an industrial systems dashboard direction: technical, tactile, dense, and memorable without becoming decorative. Benchmark panels should feel like an operations console for storage and search behavior, with strong hierarchy for scale, recall, latency, I/O, RSS, and reproducibility. Avoid marketing gloss, vague hero claims, or lightweight chart decoration.

### Design Principles
- Evidence first: benchmark claims must be backed by checked-in artifacts, explicit commands, or clearly stated local-attempt status.
- Separate measured results from targets: 1B is a benchmark target until a run completes and produces an artifact.
- Make scale legible: show how 10k, 100k, 1M, and attempted 1B relate through routing shape, segment counts, bytes, and runtime limits.
- Preserve engineering density: tables, controls, and runbooks are valuable when organized for scanning.
- Favor credibility over drama: visual impact should come from structured data and system diagrams, not unsupported claims.
